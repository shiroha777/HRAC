Federated learning enables collaborative training without centralising raw data, but it is vulnerable to Byzantine clients and model poisoning. Robust aggregation is particularly difficult under non-IID data, where benign clients may have persistent and legitimate update differences, and under temporal dynamics that allow stealthy attackers to create slow drift over many rounds.

This dissertation proposes \emph{HRAC} (History-Residual Adaptive Clipping), an oracle-free aggregation method that performs bounded suppression rather than hard exclusion. HRAC first applies a robust global norm cap based on the median update norm, then clips each client only relative to its own historical update centre. It maintains per-client exponential moving averages for residual scale and residual change, denoted by $\mu_i$ and $\nu_i$, and uses elevated late-stage $\nu_i$ values to softly reduce the weights of clients whose behaviour changes more strongly than the population baseline. Experiments across benign and adversarial settings evaluate utility, robustness, and interpretability using accuracy curves and diagnostics such as residual thresholds, $\mu/\nu$ trajectories, client weights, and attacker-aware bottom-weight summaries.
